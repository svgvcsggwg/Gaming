Chrome for Android Embraces Edge to Edge Display

By Felix
March 2, 2025

Google Chrome’s New Update Maximizes Screen Space on Android Devices

As of March 02, 2025, Google is rolling out an exciting update to its Chrome browser for Android, aimed at enhancing the user experience by making better use of screen real estate. With the release of Chrome version 135, the browser is set to utilize more space on your phone’s screen, a change that has been in testing for a select group of users and is now poised for a broader rollout. This update reflects Google’s ongoing efforts to refine its flagship browser, aligning it with modern design trends and the evolving capabilities of Android devices.

A Shift Toward Full-Screen Browsing

The Android operating system has long supported the ability for applications to extend their content across the entire screen, including areas traditionally reserved for the status bar at the top and the navigation bar at the bottom. However, many apps—including Chrome—have historically opted for a more conservative approach, leaving these system UI elements separate from the app’s content. This design choice often resulted in unused screen space, particularly on smaller devices where every pixel counts.

With the release of Android 15 in late 2024, Google introduced changes that encouraged developers to adopt a full-screen design philosophy. These updates included APIs and guidelines that made it easier for apps to draw content edge-to-edge, effectively “under” the system bars. While some applications quickly adapted to this shift, Chrome lagged slightly behind—until now. Version 135 of Chrome for Android marks a significant step forward, as Google finally embraces this full-screen capability, allowing the browser to stretch its viewport to the bottom edge of the screen.

Key Takeaway: Chrome version 135 will display web content in the background of the navigation bar, eliminating wasted space and offering a more immersive browsing experience.

What’s Changing in Chrome Version 135?

The core change in this update involves expanding Chrome’s viewport—the area where web content is rendered—to include the space typically occupied by the navigation bar. In previous versions, the navigation bar (housing the back, home, and recent apps buttons on most Android devices) sat in a fixed, opaque strip at the bottom of the screen, separate from the browser’s content. Now, Chrome will render web pages behind this bar, treating it as a semi-transparent overlay rather than a detached element.

This adjustment has a few practical implications. First, it maximizes the visible content area, which is particularly beneficial on smaller screens where vertical space is at a premium. Imagine scrolling through a news article or a social media feed without losing a chunk of the display to a static navigation bar—it’s a small but meaningful improvement. Second, the navigation bar itself becomes dynamic: it will automatically fade or slide out of view as you scroll, reappearing only when needed. This behavior mirrors the “immersive mode” seen in some Android apps and games, where system UI elements recede to prioritize content.

Visually, this update makes Chrome feel more seamless. The browser’s user interface now extends fully to the bottom of the screen, creating a cleaner, more unified look. For users accustomed to seeing a distinct separation between Chrome’s content and the Android system bars, this might take some getting used to—but the benefits are clear.

Why Now? The Context of Android 15 and Beyond

Google’s decision to implement this change in Chrome version 135 is closely tied to the broader evolution of the Android ecosystem. Android 15, released in October 2024, introduced several under-the-hood improvements aimed at enhancing app compatibility with diverse screen sizes and layouts. One of these was a stronger push toward edge-to-edge display support, which Google reinforced by updating its developer guidelines and tools. Apps that fail to adopt this approach risk looking dated or inefficient on modern devices, especially as manufacturers continue to shrink bezels and maximize screen-to-body ratios.

Chrome’s delay in adopting this feature wasn’t due to oversight but rather a deliberate pace of development. Google has been testing full-screen capabilities in Chrome for some time, with early experiments visible in beta builds as far back as mid-2024. The company likely wanted to ensure stability and compatibility across the vast array of Android devices—ranging from budget phones with modest displays to flagship models with high-resolution OLED screens—before rolling it out widely.

The timing also aligns with user expectations. As of March 2025, Android users are increasingly accustomed to apps that make full use of their screens, whether it’s video players like YouTube or productivity tools like Google Docs. Chrome, as one of the most-used apps on Android, needed to catch up to maintain its edge in a competitive browser market.

How Does This Affect Web Developers?

For the most part, this update won’t require significant changes from web developers. Google has emphasized that Chrome’s new full-screen behavior is designed to work seamlessly with existing websites. Web pages should render correctly without any adjustments, as the browser handles the integration of content with the navigation bar automatically.

However, there are edge cases to consider. Content positioned at the very bottom of a page—such as a fixed footer or a “back to top” button—might appear partially obscured behind the navigation bar in some scenarios. While the bar becomes transparent or hides during scrolling, it remains present when static, potentially overlapping with these elements. Google has advised developers to test their sites in Chrome 135 and make minor CSS tweaks if needed, such as adding padding or adjusting z-index values to ensure usability.

Fortunately, these adjustments are straightforward, and Google’s developer documentation provides clear guidance. The company’s goal is to minimize disruption while encouraging a more immersive browsing experience.

Current Rollout and Future Plans

As of March 02, 2025, the full-screen feature in Chrome 135 is still in a phased rollout. It’s currently enabled for a limited number of users, primarily those on smaller-screen devices like smartphones. Google has confirmed that tablets and other large-screen Android devices haven’t yet received this update, likely due to differences in how navigation bars function on those form factors. For example, many tablets use a gesture-based navigation system or a persistent taskbar, which may require a tailored approach.

The gradual deployment allows Google to gather feedback and address any bugs before the feature reaches all users. Based on the company’s statements, we can expect a wider release in the coming weeks or months, with large-screen support to follow in future updates—possibly Chrome 136 or later.

Looking Ahead: Google plans to extend this full-screen design to tablets and foldable devices, ensuring a consistent experience across all Android hardware.

The Bigger Picture: Enhancing User Experience

At its core, this update is about efficiency and immersion. By reclaiming the space previously reserved for the navigation bar, Chrome offers users a more engaging way to interact with the web. Whether you’re reading long-form articles, shopping online, or browsing social media, the extra screen real estate makes a difference—especially on compact devices where every inch of display matters.

Beyond aesthetics, this change reflects Google’s broader strategy to keep Chrome competitive. Rivals like Samsung Internet and Firefox have long experimented with full-screen modes, and users have come to expect modern browsers to adapt to their hardware. With this update, Chrome not only catches up but also sets a new standard for how browsers can integrate with Android’s system UI.

For now, the feature’s limited rollout means many users are still waiting to experience it firsthand. But as testing progresses and the update reaches more devices, Chrome’s evolution promises to make browsing on Android a little more seamless—and a lot more spacious.

Conclusion

Google Chrome version 135 represents a thoughtful, if overdue, enhancement to the Android browsing experience. By extending content to the bottom edge of the screen and dynamically managing the navigation bar, Chrome makes smarter use of limited display space. While the change is subtle, its impact is significant, particularly for users who value every pixel of their phone’s screen. As Google continues to refine and expand this feature, it’s clear that the future of Chrome on Android is one of greater immersion and efficiency—a win for users and developers alike.
