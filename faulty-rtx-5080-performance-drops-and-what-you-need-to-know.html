Faulty RTX 5080: Performance Drops and What You Need to Know

By Felix
March 2, 2025

The launch of Nvidia's RTX 50-series "Blackwell" graphics cards has been marred by a series of unexpected issues, raising concerns among early adopters. Initial excitement surrounding the new generation has been tempered by reports of instability, black screens, and, most recently, a manufacturing defect resulting in reduced performance due to missing Render Output Units (ROPs). This article delves into the specifics of the RTX 5080 ROP issue, its impact on gaming performance, how to check if your card is affected, and what steps you can take.

The ROP Issue: More Than Just a Minor Defect

Initially, reports surfaced concerning missing ROPs on Nvidia's GeForce RTX 5090 and RTX 5070 Ti GPUs. Nvidia acknowledged the issue, stating that it affected approximately 0.5% of those specific cards and advised affected users to seek replacements from their board manufacturers. However, the problem has since been confirmed to extend to the RTX 5080 as well. [7, 10, 11, 13, 15, 20, 23, 30]

Render Output Units (ROPs) are a crucial component of a GPU's rendering pipeline. They are responsible for the final stages of processing, handling tasks like writing pixels to the frame buffer, anti-aliasing (smoothing edges), and color blending. Having fewer ROPs than the specified amount directly impacts a card's ability to perform these operations efficiently, leading to a measurable performance decrease. [7, 21]

The faulty RTX 5080 cards have been found to have 104 ROPs instead of the advertised 112. This mirrors the issue seen with the RTX 5090 (168 instead of 176) and RTX 5070 Ti (88 instead of 96). In all cases, a single ROP partition, consisting of 8 ROP units, is missing. [1, 9, 11, 25]

Performance Impact: Variable, But Potentially Significant

Nvidia initially claimed that the average performance impact of the missing ROPs would be around 4%. While this might hold true as an overall average, independent testing by Gamers Nexus has revealed that the performance hit can be significantly higher in certain games, particularly at higher resolutions. [2, 4, 5, 8, 21]

Gamers Nexus, after putting out a bounty for a defective RTX 5080, acquired a Founders Edition model with the ROP deficiency and conducted a series of benchmark tests. [2] The results demonstrate a clear and, in some cases, considerable performance delta between a fully functional RTX 5080 and the faulty unit.

Key Findings from Gamers Nexus Testing:

  • Up to 11% performance loss in *Total War: Warhammer III* at 4K resolution. [2, 4, 5, 8]
  • 8.7% performance drop in *Dying Light 2* at 4K. This decreased to 8.8% at 1440p. [2, 4]
  • 4.9% performance difference in *Dragon's Dogma 2* at 4K. [4]
  • Minimal to no performance difference in games like *Baldur's Gate 3* and *Black Myth: Wukong*. [4]
  • In a synthetic benchmark (3DMark Time Spy), the faulty RTX 5080 scored 12% lower than a normal unit, even potentially falling behind the RTX 4080 Super in some scenarios. [2, 23]

The data clearly indicates that the impact of the missing ROPs is highly game-dependent. Games that are more heavily reliant on ROP-intensive operations, especially at higher resolutions, are more likely to exhibit a noticeable performance reduction. [2, 4]

At 1440p, the performance gap tends to be smaller, with most games falling within the 3-4% range, according to Gamers Nexus. However, even at this resolution, *Dying Light 2* still showed a substantial 8.8% difference. This suggests that the performance penalty may become more pronounced as resolutions increase, further stressing the limited ROP resources. [2, 4]

The original release date of the RTX 5080 was January 30, 2025. [3, 29] The initial MSRP was listed as $1,000. [3]

How ROPs Function, in More Detail

ROPs (Render Output Units) are crucial, specialized hardware units within a GPU that are involved with the concluding steps in transforming 3D scene information into the 2D image that is shown on a screen. Their responsibilities include:

  • Pixel Operations and Framebuffer Writes ROPs will perform a set of pixel operations, involving actions such as blending colors, executing depth checks to resolve which pixels are seen or hidden, and putting the pixels, ready to be displayed, to the buffer, awaiting them to be transmitted.
  • Multisampling Anti-Aliasing (MSAA): ROPs do this to remove the jagginess of edges, improving picture clarity by having sample several points within a single pixel. These calculations result in a smoother concluding image.
  • Memory Access: These steps write directly the frame buffer to the memory, therefore effective operations directly will dictate the card's maximum effective frame rate.

Other Blackwell Launch Issues

The ROP problem isn't the only issue to plague the RTX 50-series launch. Users have reported a range of other problems, including:

  • Black screen issues and system crashes (BSODs): These have been linked to driver instability, with Nvidia releasing driver updates (version 572.60) and vBIOS updates to address the problem. [12, 16, 17, 19] Some users have found temporary workarounds by using HDMI instead of DisplayPort or even lowering their monitor's refresh rate. [16]
  • DisplayPort connectivity issues: Some users have reported problems specifically when using DisplayPort connections, with some suggesting that HDMI 2.1 might offer a more stable experience. [16]
  • Potential PCI Express 5.0 compatibility problems: Some reports indicate stability issues when using PCIe 5.0 on certain motherboards, with downgrading to PCIe 4.0 in the BIOS sometimes providing a temporary solution. [27]

Nvidia's Response and Recommended Actions

Nvidia has officially acknowledged the ROP issue, initially stating that it affected a small percentage (0.5%) of RTX 5090, RTX 5090D, and RTX 5070 Ti GPUs. They later amended this statement to include the RTX 5080. [7, 11, 13, 15, 30] Their current statement reads:

"We have identified a rare issue affecting less than 0.5% (half a percent) of GeForce RTX 5090 / 5090D, RTX 5080, and 5070 Ti GPUs which have one fewer ROP than specified. The average graphical performance impact is 4%, with no impact on AI and Compute workloads. Affected consumers can contact the board manufacturer for a replacement. The production anomaly has been corrected." [1, 11, 30]

Nvidia GeForce Global PR director Ben Berraondo stated, "Upon further investigation, we've identified that an early production build of GeForce RTX 5080 GPUs were also affected by the same issue. Affected consumers can contact the board manufacturer for a replacement." [11, 13, 15, 30]

Nvidia places the responsibility on users to check their cards and initiate the replacement process with the board manufacturer (AIB), such as ASUS, MSI, Gigabyte, Zotac, etc. This has caused frustration among some users, especially given the difficulty and expense of acquiring these high-end cards in the first place. [5, 30]

The Zotac RTX 5080 Solid, as an example of an AIB card, has generally performed about 3-6% better in tests than the Founders Edition, however, was priced about $270-$300 more than the Founders Edition. [29]

How to Check Your RTX 50-Series Card

If you own an RTX 5090, RTX 5080, or RTX 5070 Ti, it is strongly recommended that you check your card's ROP count. The easiest way to do this is using the free utility GPU-Z from TechPowerUp. [7, 14, 25, 26]

Steps:

  1. Download and install GPU-Z from a reputable source (e.g., TechPowerUp's website).
  2. Run GPU-Z.
  3. Look for the "ROPs/TMUs" field on the main "Graphics Card" tab.
  4. Compare the number on the left (ROP count) to the expected value for your card:
    • RTX 5090: 176 ROPs [9, 14]
    • RTX 5080: 112 ROPs [9, 14]
    • RTX 5070 Ti: 96 ROPs [9]
  5. If your ROP count is lower than the expected value (168 for the 5090, 104 for the 5080, or 88 for the 5070 Ti), your card is affected and you should contact your board manufacturer for a replacement. [14, 25]

What to Do if Your Card is Affected

If you discover that your RTX 50-series card has the reduced ROP count, your primary course of action is to contact the board manufacturer (the company that made your specific card, like ASUS, Gigabyte, MSI, Zotac, etc.) and request a replacement. [1, 7, 11, 13, 15, 30] Nvidia directs affected consumers to do this, rather than handling the replacements directly. [30]

It's important to do this as soon as possible. If you are still within the return window for your purchase, returning the card to the retailer may be faster and easier than going through the manufacturer's RMA (Return Merchandise Authorization) process. [5]

Be prepared for potential delays. Given the limited availability of RTX 50-series cards, getting a replacement could take some time. [8]

Looking Ahead

While it remains uncertain, there is an expectation among certain tech analysis entities that upcoming GPU generations, notably Series 60 (anticipated to adopt 3nm process technology), could be a significant step-up from the Series 50 GPUs.

Conclusion

The ROP issue with the RTX 5080, along with other problems affecting the RTX 50-series launch, is a disappointing development for consumers who invested in Nvidia's latest generation of graphics cards. The variable performance impact, the need for users to self-diagnose, and the potential wait times for replacements all contribute to a less-than-ideal experience.

While Nvidia has acknowledged the problem and offered replacements through board manufacturers, the situation highlights the importance of thorough quality control and transparent communication, particularly in the high-end hardware market. It also emphasizes the value of independent testing and reporting by outlets like Gamers Nexus in uncovering and quantifying such issues.

If you're a Blackwell owner, check your card, and if necessary, start the replacement process. Hopefully, Nvidia and its partners will resolve these production issues swiftly and ensure that future batches of RTX 50-series cards deliver the full performance potential that customers expect.
